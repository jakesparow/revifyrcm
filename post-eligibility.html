Why 70% of denials originate at the front desk — and what an eligibility agent does about it.

An end-to-end breakdown of the eligibility-to-denial pipeline, the policy gaps real practices ignore, and a quarter of agent-floor data showing where the dollars actually leak.

SR
Sriram Raghavan · Co-founder & CTO · Apr 28, 2026

The first time we ran the analysis, I didn't believe it. Two-point-one million claims processed across thirty-eight live deployments in Q1 2026, and roughly seven out of ten denials weren't denials at all — they were eligibility errors that survived a quick check at the front desk and rode the entire pipeline downstream until a payer rejected them four to six weeks later.

The economics of that round trip are devastating. A claim worth $4,200 that denies and reworks costs an average of $181 in administrative labor by the time it's paid — assuming it's paid. Many aren't. The 2025 MGMA cost-to-collect benchmark places clean-claim recovery at 4.2% of net collections; the same claim, denied once and reworked through traditional follow-up queues, climbs to 9.1%. Twice the cost. Half the margin.

The traditional answer is to throw labor at the problem — bigger billing teams, offshore follow-up pods, more denial coordinators. We've done a different thing. We've put an agent at the front of the pipeline whose only job is to make sure the eligibility decision is right the first time. This article is what we've learned in the year since we shipped it.

Anatomy of an eligibility error

An eligibility error sounds like a clerical mistake. It almost never is. It's a policy interpretation error compounded by a workflow that doesn't surface the question to anyone qualified to answer it.

Here's a real example, slightly redacted. A 58-year-old patient presents at an orthopedic ASC for a knee arthroscopy. The front desk runs a real-time eligibility check. The payer responds with active coverage, in-network status, and a $2,500 deductible · $375 met — clean, by every definition the EHR understands.

What the front desk doesn't see, because the eligibility transaction doesn't carry it: the patient's plan has a medical-necessity carve-out for arthroscopy after age 55 unless an MRI documents specific findings within the prior 90 days. The MRI on file is from 14 months ago. The claim will be paid in full, then recouped 73 days later.

This is not a theoretical edge case. We pulled four months of denials across our orthopedic deployments and found that 32% of the dollars at risk stemmed from carve-out language that was technically available — buried in the payer policy PDF — but was never reconciled against the eligibility transaction at the time of service.

Field note

The 270/271 transaction is a starting point, not a verdict. Real eligibility lives in the payer's medical policy library, the patient's prior auth history, the network status of every CPT/place-of-service combination on the visit, and the running balance of the patient's plan-year benefit accumulators. A human can reconcile all of that in about 11 minutes per visit. A practice doesn't have 11 minutes per visit.

The eight CARC codes that account for 60% of recoverable revenue

Not all denials are recoverable. Some are correct on the merits — the service wasn't covered, the patient hadn't met deductible, the provider was out of network and there was no waiver. The interesting set is the denials that are technically wrong — where a payer's adjudication engine made a decision the contract or policy wouldn't actually support if you appealed it.

Across our production data, eight CARC codes account for 60% of dollars in this recoverable bucket. The pattern is what you'd expect — they all sit at the intersection of eligibility, prior auth, and coverage interpretation:

The denial isn't the problem. The denial is the receipt. — Sarah K., RCM Director, Charlotte hospital network

What the eligibility agent actually does

I want to be precise about what we mean by "agent" because the term has been loaded down with a year of vendor marketing. Our eligibility agent is a deterministic workflow with three LLM-powered steps and four hard-coded ones. It is not an open-ended chatbot. It does the same thing every time, in the same order, with the same audit trail.

  1. Pull the 270 transaction from the EHR or scheduling system. Hard-coded.
  2. Reconcile the 271 response against the patient's full benefit history, accumulators, and any active prior auths on file. LLM-assisted to interpret variant payer language.
  3. Look up the payer medical policy for every CPT on the visit. We maintain a vector index of 4,200+ payer policies, refreshed nightly. The agent reads the actual policy text and pulls medical-necessity criteria, age/frequency/diagnosis triggers, and any documentation requirements. LLM-driven.
  4. Verify chart documentation against the policy criteria. The agent reads the chart (with permission), looks for the specific findings the policy demands, and writes a structured determination. LLM-driven.
  5. Generate an authorization request if one is needed and not already on file. Hard-coded once the determination is made.
  6. Flag a human if the determination is ambiguous, the documentation is missing, or the dollars at risk exceed a configurable threshold. The default is $1,500.
  7. Write the audit trail. Every step, every input, every output, with a model version stamp and a citation back to the policy paragraph it relied on. Hard-coded.

The whole loop runs in 9 to 14 seconds per visit, costs us about $0.31 in inference, and replaces an 11-minute human task that no practice we've ever met actually does at scale.

What changes when you put it in front

The headline numbers across our deployments are the kind of result that, frankly, we second-guessed for the first six months until the data kept showing the same shape:

  • Eligibility-related denials drop 64% within the first 90 days.
  • Clean-claim rate moves from a typical 88–92% to 97.4%.
  • Days in A/R compresses by 9 to 14 days, depending on the specialty mix.
  • Cost-to-collect drops by 280–340 basis points. On a practice doing $30M in collections, that's $840K–$1.02M a year.

The non-headline number we care about more: humans get their attention back. The medical office staff we've spoken to don't miss the eligibility queue. They miss the version of their job where they got to talk to patients. There are still calls to make — there are always calls to make — but they're the calls where someone needs help, not the calls where we're chasing down a faxed prior auth.

The mistake to not make

The mistake we see vendors making — and we're guilty of having made it ourselves, briefly, before we corrected — is treating eligibility as an automation problem rather than a determination problem. Automating the 270/271 transaction is table stakes. Most EHRs do that already. What changes the unit economics is reading the policy, reading the chart, and writing a defensible determination with citations. That's the work. That's where the $181 goes when you don't do it.

What's next

We're now extending the same pattern upstream — into pre-scheduling. The premise is simple: if we can read the policy at time of service and avoid a denial, we can read the policy at time of booking and avoid a wasted visit. Early data from our pre-scheduling agent (we're calling it EligibilityZero) shows a 31% drop in patient-cost surprises and a measurable lift in show rate. We'll publish that in the next field note.

Until then: if you're running a billing operation and your denial rate is north of 8%, the answer is almost certainly upstream. Look at the front desk. Look at the 90 seconds before the visit started. The dollars are there.
